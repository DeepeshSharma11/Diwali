About Diwali

The Festival of Lights

Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is one of the most important festivals in Indian culture. It's a five-day celebration that marks the victory of light over darkness and good over evil.

Significance of Diwali

Diwali has different significance across various regions of India:

  • In North India, it celebrates Lord Rama's return to Ayodhya after 14 years of exile
  • In South India, it marks Lord Krishna's victory over the demon Narakasura
  • In West India, it honors the day Lord Vishnu sent the demon King Bali to rule the nether world
  • For Jains, it marks the nirvana of Lord Mahavira
  • For Sikhs, it commemorates the release of Guru Hargobind from imprisonment

Diwali Traditions

Traditional Diwali celebrations include:

  • Lighting diyas (oil lamps) and candles
  • Decorating homes with rangoli (colorful patterns)
  • Exchanging gifts and sweets with family and friends
  • Wearing new clothes
  • Performing Lakshmi Puja (worship of the goddess of wealth)
  • Bursting firecrackers (though many now opt for eco-friendly celebrations)
